Why Correlations Can’t Tell Us Whether Muscle Size Causes Strength

Part I of III — Associations and Their Discontents

causal inference
muscle physiology
statistics
simulation

Correlations between changes in muscle size and strength vary wildly across studies — anywhere from near zero to above 0.9. But this variability tells us less about biology than we might think: the same training programme, run twice, can produce almost any correlation depending on who you recruit, how you measure change, and which causal forces happen to dominate in your sample. This article uses interactive simulations to show the three structural reasons associations arise — and why none of them, on their own, can tell us whether size actually causes strength.

Published

June 13, 2026

TipKey points
  • Correlations between changes in muscle size and strength vary from near zero to above 0.9 across studies — and that variability is itself the problem, not the answer.
  • A high correlation can emerge even when size has no causal effect on strength whatsoever, through at least three distinct structural mechanisms.
  • Even when the causal effect is real, the correlation you observe depends heavily on choices made before the analysis — who you recruit, how you measure change, and what scale you report it on.
  • Establishing that size causes strength requires a different framework entirely, which we introduce in Part II.

You have probably heard that correlation is not causation. It is one of those phrases that gets repeated so often it has almost lost its meaning — a disclaimer people attach to findings before moving on to interpret them causally anyway. But if everyone knows this, why do we keep reaching for correlations when we want to understand whether muscle growth drives strength gains?

I think the honest answer is that we are trying to answer a causal question and do not always know how to do it properly. Correlations are easy to compute, easy to report, and — crucially — easy to misread as evidence that one thing is causing another. The literature on muscle size and strength is a good example: observed correlations between changes in the two vary anywhere from near zero to above 0.9 across studies, and that variability itself is rarely treated as the puzzle it should be.

The deeper problem is not which correlation we pick, or how cleverly we estimate it. It is that no correlation, however well-constructed, can answer the question we actually care about: does gaining muscle make you stronger?

That question is causal. Correlations are not. This is not a philosophical distinction — it has real consequences for how we interpret training research and design interventions. In this article, we use interactive simulations to make those consequences concrete. We first show how a strong association between size and strength changes can emerge even when one has nothing to do with the other. We then show how, even in a world where size genuinely does cause strength, the number we report can change dramatically depending on choices that have nothing to do with the underlying biology.

Three Ways Associations Arise

Association: two variables are associated if information about one tells you something about the likelihood of the other. A correlation coefficient measures this. It says nothing about why.

Every systematic association between muscle size (\(M\)) and strength (\(S\)) traces back to one of three causal structures — or, in small samples, to chance alone Hernan and Robins (2026). We can represent these structures with directed acyclic graphs (DAGs), where an arrow from \(A\) to \(B\) means “\(A\) is a cause of \(B\).” No arrow means no direct causal relationship. What makes DAGs useful is that they let us read off, just from the diagram, which variables we would expect to be associated — and why. Below we walk through each scenario in turn.

Reason 1 — Direct Causation

M = Muscle Size · S = Strength

In this scenario, muscle growth directly causes strength to increase. This is what we would generally expect: when one variable has a causal effect on another, we should also expect the two to be associated. A correlation between \(\Delta M\) and \(\Delta S\) is therefore telling you something real — the two are linked because one drives the other.

A helpful way to think about DAGs is to imagine the arrows as pipes through which association can flow. If two variables are connected by a pipe, they will tend to move together. In this scenario, M and S are connected by a single direct pipe — so when M goes up, S tends to go up too. The trouble, as we will see, is that pipes can carry association between variables even when neither causes the other.

Reason 2 — Common Cause (Confounding)

T drives both M and S independently — their correlation carries no causal meaning

In this scenario, neither M nor S causes the other. Instead, both are driven by a common cause — training. Because both variables share the same upstream cause, they will tend to move together in any dataset, even without a direct connection between them.

This means that if you learn someone gained a lot of muscle over a training block, you would reasonably update your expectation of their strength gains upward — not because muscle caused the strength gain, but because both are downstream of the same training stimulus. A correlation between \(\Delta M\) and \(\Delta S\) will appear, and it may be substantial, even when the direct effect of M on S is exactly zero.

Reason 3 — Collider Bias

P is a collider — both M and S cause P, so conditioning on P opens a spurious path between them

In this scenario, M and S are again causally unrelated — and unlike Scenario 2, they do not even share a common cause. Both independently influence a third variable: whether a participant completes the study. Someone who gained little muscle might still complete the programme because their strength progressed well enough to keep them motivated, and vice versa. Study completion is therefore a common effect of both M and S — a collider.

Here is the key insight: in the full population, M and S are independent. Learning that someone gained a lot of muscle tells you nothing about their strength gains, because there is no pipe connecting them — the only path runs through the collider, and colliders, unlike common causes, block the flow of association rather than transmitting it.

But the moment we restrict our analysis to completers — conditioning on the collider — that blockage disappears. Within completers, those who gained little muscle must have gained more strength to make the cut, and those who gained little strength must have gained more muscle. A spurious association between M and S appears entirely as an artefact of who ended up in the analysed sample, not because of anything happening biologically between the two variables.

server <- function(input, output, session) {

  current_world <- reactiveVal("direct")

  # Load preset when button clicked
  observeEvent(input$preset, {
    p <- presets[[input$preset]]
    current_world(input$preset)
    session$sendCustomMessage("highlight_btn", paste0("btn_", input$preset))

    if (input$preset != "collider") {
      updateSliderInput(session, "b_ts", value = p$b_ts)
      updateSliderInput(session, "b_sf", value = p$b_sf)
      updateSliderInput(session, "b_tf", value = p$b_tf)
      updateSliderInput(session, "sig",  value = p$sig)
    }
  })

  output$scenario_subtitle <- renderUI({
    p <- presets[[current_world()]]
    div(class="scenario-subtitle", p$subtitle)
  })

  # ── Data generation ──────────────────────────────────────────
  dat <- reactive({
    input$resample
    input$preset
    n    <- input$n
    b_ts <- input$b_ts
    b_sf <- input$b_sf
    b_tf <- input$b_tf
    sig  <- input$sig
    world <- current_world()

    if (world == "direct") {
      training <- rnorm(n)
      dM <- b_ts * training + sig * rnorm(n)
      dS <- b_sf * dM + b_tf * training + sig * rnorm(n)
      selected <- rep(TRUE, n)

    } else if (world == "confound") {
      training <- rnorm(n)
      dM <- b_ts * training + sig * rnorm(n)
      dS <- b_tf * training + sig * rnorm(n)   # M → S path is zero
      selected <- rep(TRUE, n)

    } else {
      # Collider: powerlifting selection
      # M and S are generated independently
      # Both contribute to "making it" as a competitive powerlifter
      dM <- rnorm(n)
      dS <- rnorm(n)
      # Selection score: high muscle AND high strength → compete
      logit_select <- 1.2 * dM + 1.2 * dS - 0.5 + 0.4 * rnorm(n)
      prob_select  <- 1 / (1 + exp(-logit_select))
      selected     <- runif(n) < prob_select
    }

    data.frame(dM=dM, dS=dS, selected=selected)
  })

  # ── Plot ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  output$scatter <- renderPlot({
    d     <- dat()
    world <- current_world()

    if (world == "collider") {
      d_sel   <- d[d$selected,  ]
      d_unsel <- d[!d$selected, ]
      r_val   <- if (nrow(d_sel) > 2) cor(d_sel$dM, d_sel$dS) else NA

      ggplot() +
        geom_point(data=d_unsel, aes(x=dM, y=dS),
                   colour="#555555", alpha=0.25, size=1.5) +
        geom_point(data=d_sel, aes(x=dM, y=dS),
                   colour="#e05c5c", alpha=0.55, size=2) +
        geom_smooth(data=d_sel, aes(x=dM, y=dS),
                    method="lm", se=TRUE,
                    colour="#e05c5c", fill="#e05c5c",
                    alpha=0.15, linetype="dashed", linewidth=0.9) +
        labs(
          x = "Muscle size (M)",
          y = "Strength (S)",
          title = sprintf("Observed r = %.2f  (selected only)",
                          ifelse(is.na(r_val), NA, r_val)),
          subtitle = sprintf("N total = %d  |  N selected (competing) = %d  |  N excluded = %d",
                             nrow(d), sum(d$selected), sum(!d$selected))
        ) +
        theme_dark_custom()

    } else {
      r_val <- cor(d$dM, d$dS)
      ggplot(d, aes(x=dM, y=dS)) +
        geom_point(colour="#5b9ec9", alpha=0.40, size=1.8) +
        geom_smooth(method="lm", se=TRUE,
                    colour="#e05c5c", fill="#e05c5c",
                    alpha=0.15, linetype="dashed", linewidth=0.9) +
        labs(
          x = "Δ Muscle size (M)",
          y = "Δ Strength (S)",
          title = sprintf("Observed r = %.2f", r_val),
          subtitle = sprintf("N = %d", nrow(d))
        ) +
        theme_dark_custom()
    }
  }, bg="#1e1e1e")

  # ── Stats ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  output$r_obs <- renderText({
    d     <- dat()
    world <- current_world()
    d_plot <- if (world == "collider") d[d$selected, ] else d
    if (nrow(d_plot) < 3) return("—")
    sprintf("%.2f", cor(d_plot$dM, d_plot$dS))
  })

  output$causal_share <- renderUI({
    world <- current_world()
    if (world == "direct") {
      total <- input$b_sf^2 + input$b_tf^2 + input$sig^2 + 1e-4
      pct   <- round(100 * input$b_sf^2 / total)
      div(class="stat-value green", paste0(pct, "%"))
    } else if (world == "confound") {
      div(class="stat-value red", "0%")
    } else {
      div(class="stat-value", style="color:#888;font-size:1rem",
          "0% — M and S are independent")
    }
  })

  output$world_label <- renderText({
    switch(current_world(),
      direct   = "Direct causation",
      confound = "Confounding",
      collider = "Collider / Selection"
    )
  })
}

shinyApp(ui, server)
TipWhat to look for
  • Scenario 1: Even when M genuinely causes S, the observed correlation reflects more than just that causal effect — training is simultaneously pushing both variables up, inflating the association beyond what the M→S arrow alone would produce. The causal share displayed is the fraction of variance in S attributable to M in this simplified model — an upper bound, not a precise estimate.
  • Scenario 2: The Size → Strength effect is set to zero — yet a meaningful correlation appears, driven entirely by training as a shared upstream cause. Try resampling several times to see how stable it is.
  • Scenario 3: Grey points are the full population — M and S are completely independent. Red points are athletes who made it into a competitive powerlifting dataset. The regression line is fitted to red points only. A spurious association emerges purely from who got selected, not from any biological relationship between the two variables.

Conclusion

The three scenarios above share a common thread: in each one, a researcher running a standard correlation analysis would observe an association between \(\Delta M\) and \(\Delta S\) and face the same temptation — to read it as evidence that muscle growth drives strength gains. In Scenario 1, that interpretation would be directionally correct but would still overstate the isolated contribution of size. In Scenarios 2 and 3, it would be entirely wrong.

This is not a criticism of the researchers who run these studies. Most papers in this literature are careful — they report associations and note, somewhere in the discussion, that causation cannot be inferred. The problem is that the moment a finding enters circulation, the causal reading tends to follow automatically. We see a correlation of 0.9 and hear that muscle size drives strength. The disclaimer quietly disappears.

What we need is not more careful disclaimers. We need a framework that makes explicit what we are assuming when we move from an observed association to a causal claim — and that allows us to ask whether those assumptions are reasonable given what we know about how training adaptations work. That framework exists, and it is the subject of Part II.
