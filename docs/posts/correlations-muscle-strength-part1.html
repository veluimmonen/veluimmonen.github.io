Why Correlations Can’t Tell Us Whether Muscle Size Causes Strength

Part I of III — Associations and Their Discontents

causal inference
muscle physiology
statistics
simulation

A recent analysis found r = 0.92 between hypertrophy and strength gains. But high correlations have many parents — and causation is only one of them. This article uses interactive R simulations to show exactly how associations arise, and why they systematically mislead.

Published

June 13, 2026

Greg Nuckols recently wrote a careful analysis of whether hypertrophy and strength gains are more strongly correlated than we previously thought. His conclusion — that within-person repeated-measures correlations of r ≈ 0.90 look impressive, but the between-person picture is far murkier — is exactly right. But I think the deeper problem is not which correlation coefficient we pick. It is that no correlation coefficient, however cleverly estimated, can answer the causal question we actually care about: does gaining muscle make you stronger?

That question is causal. Correlations are not. The gap between those two things is not a pedantic statistical quibble — it produces completely different answers in practice, and this article will show you why, using interactive simulations you can run right here in your browser (no server required).

We will work through three mechanisms that produce associations between variables that have nothing to do with one causing the other. Then we will add a second layer of trouble: even when we correctly identify that A causes B, our estimate of the effect’s size will depend entirely on whether the underlying biological process is additive or multiplicative — and which scale (absolute vs. percentage change) we happen to look at.

NoteHow to read this article

Each section has an interactive simulation powered by R running directly in your browser via shinylive. Adjust the sliders to change the true causal structure of a simulated world, and the plots show what a researcher would observe if they ran a correlation study in that world.

Part I — Three Ways Associations Arise

Every association between two variables — muscle size (S) and strength (F) — traces back to one of three causal structures. We can draw these with directed acyclic graphs (DAGs): arrows represent direct causal effects; the absence of an arrow means no direct effect (conditional on everything else in the graph).

Structure 1 — Direct Causation

Training ──► Size (S) ──► Strength (F)
                 ↑
          (also Training ──► F directly, via neural adaptation)

Training causes muscle to grow, and that growth directly causes strength to increase. A correlation between ΔS and ΔF is telling you something real — but only that the effect exists, not how large it is relative to all other pathways.

Structure 2 — Common Cause (Confounding)

         Training
        ↙         ↘
   Size (S)     Strength (F)
        ↘         ↙
     (spurious association)

Training causes both muscle growth and strength gains independently — through neural adaptations, tendon stiffness changes, motor learning. Size and strength will be correlated in any dataset, but removing the muscle growth (magically) would not reduce strength gains at all. The correlation is real; the causal interpretation is wrong.

Structure 3 — Collider Bias (Selection)

Size (S) ──►  Progress score  ◄── Strength (F)
              (conditioning here…)
              (…opens a spurious path between S and F)

Suppose a study retains only “good responders” — participants above a threshold on an overall progress metric that combines size and strength gains. Both contribute to that selection criterion. Conditioning on it — or restricting analysis to participants above the threshold — opens a spurious path between Size and Strength even if they have zero direct causal relationship. Within the selected group, those who grew more muscle appear to have gained more strength, simply because both had to be elevated to survive selection.

WarningThe within-person correlation and confounding

The within-person repeated-measures correlation celebrated by Marques et al. (2025) is actually more susceptible to the confounding story, not less. Within each person, muscle size and neural/tendon adaptations are both driven by the same training stimulus, on the same timeline, within the same body. A strong within-person track of both variables rising together is exactly what you would predict from the confounding DAG. It does not require direct causation.

server <- function(input, output, session) {
  set.seed(NULL)

  dat <- reactive({
    input$resample
    n   <- input$n
    b_ts <- input$trainSize
    b_sf <- input$sizeStr
    b_tf <- input$trainStr
    sig  <- input$noise
    training <- rnorm(n)

    if (input$world == "direct") {
      dS <- b_ts * training + sig * rnorm(n)
      dF <- b_sf * dS + b_tf * training + sig * rnorm(n)
      selected <- rep(TRUE, n)
    } else if (input$world == "confound") {
      dS <- b_ts * training + sig * rnorm(n)
      dF <- b_tf * training + sig * rnorm(n)   # no size → strength
      selected <- rep(TRUE, n)
    } else {
      # collider: size and strength from independent causes
      dS <- b_ts * rnorm(n) + sig * rnorm(n)
      dF <- b_tf * rnorm(n) + sig * rnorm(n)
      score    <- dS + dF
      threshold <- quantile(score, 0.40)
      selected <- score >= threshold
    }
    data.frame(dS=dS, dF=dF, selected=selected)
  })

  output$scatter <- renderPlot({
    d <- dat()
    d_plot <- if (input$world == "collider") d[d$selected, ] else d

    r_val <- cor(d_plot$dS, d_plot$dF)

    ggplot(d_plot, aes(x=dS, y=dF)) +
      geom_point(
        aes(colour = if(input$world=="collider") "Selected" else "All"),
        alpha=0.45, size=2
      ) +
      geom_smooth(method="lm", se=TRUE, colour="#c0392b",
                  fill="#e74c3c", alpha=0.15, linetype="dashed", linewidth=0.8) +
      scale_colour_manual(values=c("All"="#2c6e8a","Selected"="#c0392b"),
                          guide="none") +
      labs(
        x = "Δ Muscle size", y = "Δ Strength",
        title = sprintf("Observed r = %.2f", r_val)
      ) +
      theme_minimal(base_size=13) +
      theme(plot.title = element_text(face="bold", colour="#1a1a1a"))
  })

  output$r_obs <- renderText({
    d <- dat()
    d_plot <- if (input$world == "collider") d[d$selected, ] else d
    sprintf("%.2f", cor(d_plot$dS, d_plot$dF))
  })

  output$r_causal <- renderText({
    if (input$world == "direct") {
      total <- input$sizeStr^2 + input$trainStr^2 + input$noise^2 + 1e-4
      sprintf("%.0f%%", 100 * input$sizeStr^2 / total)
    } else if (input$world == "confound") {
      "0% (none)"
    } else {
      "Undefined"
    }
  })

  output$world_label <- renderText({
    switch(input$world,
      direct   = "Direct cause",
      confound = "Confounding",
      collider = "Collider"
    )
  })
}

shinyApp(ui, server)

Key insight: In the confounding world, set Size → Strength to 0 and watch the correlation remain comfortably above r = 0.3–0.5, driven entirely by training as a shared cause. In the collider world, Size and Strength are generated independently — yet restricting to the top 60% of progress scores conjures an association from nothing.

Part II — The Scale Problem: Additive vs. Multiplicative Worlds

Suppose we grant, for the sake of argument, that muscle size truly causes strength — Structure 1 is the true DAG. We would still face a second, independent problem: we do not know the functional form of the biological relationship.

Consider two hypothetical worlds:

World A — Additive: Each additional cm³ of muscle adds the same absolute number of Newtons, regardless of starting size. The relationship between ΔSize and ΔStrength is linear on absolute scales.

World B — Multiplicative: Each additional cm³ of muscle adds the same percentage of current force production. Somebody who starts stronger gains more absolute Newtons from the same volume growth than somebody who starts weaker.

Both are biologically plausible — the truth is probably somewhere between them. But they have radically different implications for what a correlation study shows, depending on which scale the researcher chooses.

Why the scale of measurement changes everything

Researchers routinely express both hypertrophy and strength gains as percentage changes from baseline — partly for comparability. But in a multiplicative world, percentage changes on both sides correlate strongly with each other even if the absolute relationship is noisy. In an additive world, using percentage changes introduces a spurious association driven by baseline values: someone who starts small gains a large percentage from a small absolute gain, while a large person’s identical absolute gain registers as a small percentage. The correlation you observe is partly about the biology, and partly about where your participants happened to start.

NoteThe Marques et al. r = 0.92 through this lens

The within-person r = 0.92 uses percentage changes as the outcome. In a multiplicative world with within-person comparison (which is guaranteed — each person is compared to themselves), baseline-size noise that would inflate the percentage-change correlation between subjects is already perfectly controlled. The high r is more informative about the data structure than about the causal magnitude.

server <- function(input, output, session) {

  sim2 <- reactive({
    input$resample2
    n   <- input$n2
    b   <- input$beta
    bv  <- input$baseVar
    sig <- input$noise2

    S0  <- pmax(0.2, 1 + bv * rnorm(n))
    F0  <- pmax(0.2, 1 + bv * rnorm(n))

    dS_abs <- pmax(0.01, 0.1 + 0.05 * rnorm(n) + sig * 0.03 * rnorm(n))

    if (input$dgp == "additive") {
      dF_abs <- b * dS_abs + sig * 0.08 * rnorm(n)
    } else {
      pct_s  <- dS_abs / S0
      pct_f  <- b * pct_s + sig * 0.05 * rnorm(n)
      dF_abs <- pct_f * F0
    }

    data.frame(
      dS_abs = dS_abs,
      dF_abs = dF_abs,
      pct_dS = dS_abs / S0,
      pct_dF = dF_abs / pmax(0.01, F0)
    )
  })

  output$two_plots <- renderPlot({
    d <- sim2()
    r_abs <- cor(d$dS_abs, d$dF_abs)
    r_pct <- cor(d$pct_dS, d$pct_dF)

    p1 <- ggplot(d, aes(dS_abs, dF_abs)) +
      geom_point(colour="#2c6e8a", alpha=0.35, size=1.8) +
      geom_smooth(method="lm", se=TRUE, colour="#c0392b",
                  fill="#e74c3c", alpha=0.12, linetype="dashed", linewidth=0.8) +
      labs(x="Δ Size (absolute)", y="Δ Strength (absolute)",
           title=sprintf("Absolute  —  r = %.2f", r_abs)) +
      theme_minimal(base_size=12) +
      theme(plot.title=element_text(face="bold", colour="#2c6e8a"))

    p2 <- ggplot(d, aes(pct_dS, pct_dF)) +
      geom_point(colour="#2d7d46", alpha=0.35, size=1.8) +
      geom_smooth(method="lm", se=TRUE, colour="#c0392b",
                  fill="#e74c3c", alpha=0.12, linetype="dashed", linewidth=0.8) +
      labs(x="% Δ Size", y="% Δ Strength",
           title=sprintf("Percentage  —  r = %.2f", r_pct)) +
      theme_minimal(base_size=12) +
      theme(plot.title=element_text(face="bold", colour="#2d7d46"))

    p1 + p2
  })

  output$r_abs  <- renderText({ d<-sim2(); sprintf("%.2f", cor(d$dS_abs,d$dF_abs)) })
  output$r_pct  <- renderText({ d<-sim2(); sprintf("%.2f", cor(d$pct_dS,d$pct_dF)) })
  output$r_gap  <- renderText({
    d<-sim2()
    sprintf("%.2f", abs(cor(d$dS_abs,d$dF_abs) - cor(d$pct_dS,d$pct_dF)))
  })
  output$beta_out <- renderText({ sprintf("%.2f", input$beta) })
}

shinyApp(ui, server)

In the additive world, push baseline variability to 0.8 and watch the percentage-change correlation diverge from the absolute one — the two are measuring different things. In the multiplicative world, the roles reverse. The biology did not change; only the analyst’s choice of scale did.

Part III — Putting It Together: The Identification Problem

Even if we know the true causal structure and the correct scale, a further problem remains: a wide range of true causal worlds can produce the same observed correlation. The simulation below lets you adjust all parameters simultaneously. The grey band marks r ∈ [0.85, 0.95] — roughly what Marques et al. observed. See how many distinct causal configurations land inside it.

server <- function(input, output, session) {

  sim3 <- reactive({
    input$resample3
    n    <- input$s3_n
    b_d  <- input$s3_direct
    b_c  <- input$s3_common
    sig  <- input$s3_noise
    sc   <- input$s3_scale
    bv   <- input$s3_bv

    training <- rnorm(n)
    S0 <- pmax(0.2, 1 + bv * rnorm(n))
    F0 <- pmax(0.2, 1 + bv * rnorm(n))

    dS_abs <- pmax(0.01, b_c * training * 0.15 + 0.08 + sig * 0.05 * rnorm(n))
    dF_direct  <- b_d * dS_abs
    dF_common  <- b_c * training * 0.15
    dF_abs <- dF_direct + dF_common + sig * 0.05 * rnorm(n)

    # blend between absolute and % scale
    x_mix <- (1 - sc) * dS_abs + sc * (dS_abs / S0)
    y_mix <- (1 - sc) * dF_abs + sc * (dF_abs / pmax(0.01, F0))

    list(
      x = x_mix, y = y_mix,
      causal_frac  = b_d^2 / (b_d^2 + b_c^2 + sig^2 + 1e-4),
      confound_frac = b_c^2 / (b_d^2 + b_c^2 + sig^2 + 1e-4)
    )
  })

  output$scatter3 <- renderPlot({
    s   <- sim3()
    r   <- cor(s$x, s$y)
    d   <- data.frame(x=s$x, y=s$y)

    # y-range for target band
    yr  <- range(s$y)
    band_lo <- yr[1] + 0.85 * diff(yr)
    band_hi <- yr[1] + 0.95 * diff(yr)

    ggplot(d, aes(x, y)) +
      annotate("rect",
               xmin=-Inf, xmax=Inf,
               ymin=band_lo, ymax=band_hi,
               fill="grey80", alpha=0.4) +
      annotate("text", x=min(d$x)+0.01*diff(range(d$x)),
               y=mean(c(band_lo,band_hi)),
               label="target r ∈ [0.85, 0.95]",
               hjust=0, size=3, colour="grey50") +
      geom_point(colour="#2c6e8a", alpha=0.35, size=1.8) +
      geom_smooth(method="lm", se=TRUE,
                  colour="#c0392b", fill="#e74c3c",
                  alpha=0.12, linetype="dashed", linewidth=0.8) +
      labs(x = ifelse(input$s3_scale < 0.5, "Δ Size (absolute mix)", "% Δ Size (pct mix)"),
           y = ifelse(input$s3_scale < 0.5, "Δ Strength (absolute mix)", "% Δ Strength (pct mix)"),
           title = sprintf("Observed r = %.2f", r)) +
      theme_minimal(base_size=13) +
      theme(plot.title=element_text(face="bold",colour="#1a1a1a"))
  })

  output$r3_obs     <- renderText({ sprintf("%.2f", cor(sim3()$x, sim3()$y)) })
  output$r3_causal  <- renderText({ sprintf("%.0f%%", 100 * sim3()$causal_frac) })
  output$r3_confound <- renderText({ sprintf("%.0f%%", 100 * sim3()$confound_frac) })

  output$target_msg <- renderUI({
    r <- cor(sim3()$x, sim3()$y)
    in_target <- r >= 0.85 & r <= 0.95
    div(id="target_msg",
        style=paste0("background:", if(in_target) "#d5f5e3" else "#fdecea",
                     ";color:", if(in_target) "#1e8449" else "#922b21", ";"),
        if(in_target) "✓ In target zone" else "✗ Outside target"
    )
  })
}

You should find it easy to achieve r > 0.85 even with the direct effect at zero, by increasing the common-cause coefficient and switching to percentage scaling. Conversely, a substantial direct effect can yield r ≈ 0.3 if baseline variability is high and you measure absolute changes in a multiplicative world. The same number can arise from very different data-generating processes.

What Would Actually Help?

To answer whether muscle size causally affects strength, we need one of the following:

1. Experimental variation in size independent of training load. If you could randomly assign participants to gain different amounts of muscle (via BFR, very different rep ranges equated for effort, or pharmacological aids) while holding training volume and motor learning constant, you could estimate the causal effect. Some designs come close, but unmeasured confounders remain.

2. A formal causal mediation analysis — which attempts to decompose the total effect of training into the portion that runs through muscle growth and the portion that bypasses it (neural adaptations, etc.).

Crucially, however, even causal mediation analysis makes strong assumptions that are unlikely to hold perfectly. Part II of this series will dig into the mediation framework, and Part III will show exactly where those assumptions break — and what happens to our estimates when they do.

Summary

Problem Mechanism Fix
Confounding Training drives both size and strength Randomise size independently of training
Collider bias Selecting on a common effect of both Avoid conditioning on downstream variables

Identification Many causal worlds → same r Need causal assumptions, not just data

Correlations between hypertrophy and strength gains arise from at least three structural mechanisms, any of which can produce arbitrarily high observed correlations. No correlation statistic — Pearson, repeated-measures, or otherwise — can distinguish between them without additional assumptions about the causal graph.

Beyond causal structure, the scale of measurement interacts with the data-generating process to produce correlations that reflect the researcher’s analytic choices as much as the underlying biology.

The conclusion is not that muscle size does not cause strength — it clearly contributes. The conclusion is that the correlation coefficient is a blunt, causally agnostic tool that cannot tell us how much it contributes, under what conditions, or for whom. For that, we need a different framework.

That is where we are going next.

Simulations are stochastic; resample to build intuition across runs. All data are synthetic.
